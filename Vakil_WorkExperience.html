As a Dublin High School student, I have had the opportunity to go on many field trips to technical companies and STEM oriented colleges. In addition, several guest speakers from STEM fields have come in to talk to us during school. These have all been arranged in an effort to get us to think about the diversity of engineering careers offered and what we may want to pursue in the future. I have been to one field trip as an Engineering Student. It was the Patriot Jet Team trip during my sophomore year. This field trip was incredibly memorable because of the fascinating facts that they provided to us, and the engaging demonstrations that they utilized to help us understand concepts. I never knew before that some airplanes could break the sound barrier, going over 760 miles per hour. To go faster than sound seemed crazy to me. When someone speaks, you can hear them practically instantaneously. Yet airplanes can go faster than that. In addition, another fact that in the moment seemed to simultaneously contradict what I have been taught, and yet make complete sense, was that airplanes have redundant features. At first this seemed like a joke. In Computer Science/Software Engineering it was drilled into me that redundancies in code are bad. You want your code to be concise. Yet if you think about it logically, airplanes must be redundant. They need to have backup plans because if anything goes wrong it can be life-threateningly dangerous. There must be a fail-safe option. In addition, two things that I had never thought about before, but made complete sense once told to me, were that airplanes move faster when their external gasoline tanks are pulled in and that aircrafts take off against the wind. I was able to tie these concepts back to everyday occurrences. Having figure skated before, it makes sense that the airplane moves faster once the gasoline tanks are pulled in because I spin faster when I pull in my arms. My arms, just like the gasoline tanks, are generally farther from my body to provide stability. In addition, kites take off into the wind. The same aerodynamic principle must also apply to airplanes, as they both fly.

My mentor, Howard Hirano, has also done a great deal to increase my exposure to various engineering disciplines. He introduced me to several of his old coworkers at Lawrence Livermore and Sandia Laboratories. This not only helped me see that countless jobs exist in intersection with the field of engineering, it also helped push me out of my comfort zone and grow more comfortable communicating professionally with people. Howard also provided me with valuable insights on how to approach interview situations. He helped me see that during an interview the candidate is there to assess if the job is right for them just as much as the company is there to determine whether they are a right fit for the group. The interviewers should not be the only ones collecting data from the interaction. An interviewee should always prepare beforehand with notes and questions to maximize what they get out of an interview. Ultimately, Howard gave me numerous tips like these and showed me how to holistically examine my life and the career path that I am shaping for myself. It is a practice that I am very grateful to have learnt, and one that I have now come to use almost subliminally.
